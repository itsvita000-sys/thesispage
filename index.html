CH. 1 Introduction
▾
1.1Background of the Study
  • Post-COVID shift: rapid growth of remote interpreting & online conferencing
  • ASR technology integrated into Zoom, Google Meet, Webex → live captions during interpreting
  • CAI tools include: terminology databases, note-taking tools, ASR systems
  • Two-sided effect: subtitles may help with numbers/names/accents BUT also add new input channels → cognitive overload risk (Seeber, 2017)
  • Interpreters' inconsistent perceptions of ASR: some find it helpful, some find it distracting (Wan, 2022; Wang et al., 2025)
1.2Multimodal Processing & Cognitive Load in Interpreting
  • Interpreters must simultaneously: listen → retain → produce
  • Adding visual text creates third channel → attention allocation challenge
  • Eye-tracking studies show attention split between speaker and captions (Seeber, 2011)
  • ASR accuracy issues: unreliable text may cause extra cognitive effort to "verify" (Wang et al., 2025)
1.3Research Gap Gap
  • Most empirical studies focus on simultaneous interpreting — consecutive interpreting underexplored
  • Different subtitle language types not systematically compared
  • Cognitive load + subtitle modality = rarely studied together
  • Training level differences in subtitle use = unexplored
1.4Research Purpose & Research Questions Core
  • RQ1: Effects of subtitle modalities on interpreting performance (accuracy, terminology, fluency, delivery)
  • RQ2: Effects of subtitle modalities on cognitive load
  • RQ3: Do training levels (beginner vs. intermediate) moderate the above effects?
  • 4 subtitle conditions as a linguistic progression: No subtitle → EN → Bilingual → ZH
1.5Significance of the Study
  • Extends multimodal processing literature into consecutive interpreting + ASR context
  • Practical: informs technology-assisted interpreter training curriculum
CH. 2 Literature Review
▾
2.1Interpreting as a Cognitive Process
  • Interpreting ≠ translation: real-time, no revision, transient input (Pöchhacker, 2004)
  • Multiple simultaneous processes: comprehension → memory → production → monitoring
  • Meaning-oriented, not form-oriented → requires pragmatic adjustment
2.1.1 Characteristics of Interpreting
  • Real-time; transient spoken input (can't go back)
  • Simultaneous coordination of listening, memory, production
  • Constrained by human cognitive capacity
2.1.2 Gile's Effort Model (1995, 2009) Key Theory
  • L (Listening & Analysis) + M (Memory) + P (Production) + C (Coordination)
  • "Tightrope hypothesis": interpreters often operate near cognitive capacity limit
  • Overload → omissions, simplifications, errors
  • Adding subtitle channel increases demand on L and C efforts
2.2Cognitive Load in Interpreting
2.2.1 Cognitive Load Theory — Sweller (1988) Key Theory
  • Working memory: limited capacity + duration
  • 3 types: Intrinsic load (task difficulty) / Extraneous load (presentation format) / Germane load (schema building)
  • Subtitles may add extraneous load if they compete with auditory processing
2.2.2 Cognitive Load in Interpreting
  • Interpreting = one of highest cognitive load tasks in language activity
  • Tightrope hypothesis: small increases in difficulty → performance drops
2.2.3 Factors Affecting Cognitive Load
  • Speech rate: faster = more load (Gile, 2009; Yang, 2023)
  • Information density: complex syntax/terminology = more load (Moser-Mercer, 2000)
  • Multimodal input: visual text = additional attentional demand (Seeber, 2011, 2017)
2.3Multimodal Processing in Interpreting Key Theory
2.3.1 Audiovisual Speech Perception
  • Sumby & Pollack (1954): visual lip movements improve comprehension in noise
  • McGurk effect (1976): audio + visual are integrated, not just additive
2.3.2 Multimodal Input in Interpreting (Seeber, 2017)
  • Interpreting = multimodal cognitive activity (audio + visual + own output)
  • Attention must be allocated across channels simultaneously
  • Aligned inputs → may support comprehension; competing inputs → interference
2.3.3 Audiovisual Discrepancies
  • Wang et al. (2024): audio-visual mismatches negatively affect performance in ASR-assisted SI
  • Yu (2024): discrepancies disrupt processing → increased difficulty & errors
  • ASR errors = frequent source of discrepancy
2.4Technology in Interpreting
2.4.1 Computer-Assisted Interpreting (CAI) Tools
  • Includes: terminology tools, glossaries, note-taking apps, ASR systems (Fantinuoli, 2018)
  • CAI reshapes how cognitive resources are managed during task
2.4.2 ASR in Interpreting
  • ASR converts speech to text in real time; growing use in RSI (Pöchhacker & Liu, 2024)
  • Can help with terminology, names, numbers (Li, 2013; Tammasrisawat & Rangponsumrit, 2023)
  • Raises question: how do interpreters integrate text + audio channels?
2.4.3 Effects of ASR-Generated Subtitles
  • Wang et al. (2025): trainees find ASR helpful for terminology & verification
  • But: when errors occur → must decide to trust ASR or auditory input = extra load
  • Both facilitative AND disruptive effects documented
2.5Subtitle Support & Interpreting Performance
2.5.1 Textual Support in Interpreting
  • Written text alongside speech → dual-channel access to information
  • Can confirm names, numbers, terminology (Díaz-Cintas & Remael, 2021)
2.5.2 Multimodal Input & Attention Allocation
  • Seeber (2017): interpreters must manage competing streams of input
  • Visual text forces shift between listening mode and reading mode
  • If streams are aligned: reinforces comprehension; if mismatched: interference
2.5.3 Previous Empirical Studies
  • Wang et al. (2025): trainees perceive ASR as helpful; reliance varies with ASR quality
  • Tammasrisawat & Rangponsumrit (2023): ASR-CAI tools may improve SI accuracy
  • Yu (2024): discrepancies disrupt processing and increase cognitive burden
2.6Research Gap (Summary) Gap
  • Studies mainly on simultaneous interpreting; consecutive = understudied
  • No systematic comparison of subtitle language type (EN vs. Bilingual vs. ZH)
  • Subtitle modality + cognitive load not empirically tested together
  • Training level as moderating variable = unstudied
  • This study: first to address all 4 gaps in a single consecutive interpreting experiment
CH. 3 Methodology
▾
3.1Research Design Design
  • Mixed-methods experimental design: within-subject repeated measures
  • Quantitative + qualitative triangulation
  • 4 subtitle conditions, fixed order — rationale: progression from no support → source-lang → bilingual → target-lang
  • Two dependent variables: interpreting performance + cognitive load
  • Between-subjects factor: training level (beginner vs. intermediate)
3.2Participants N=14
  • 14 student interpreters, graduate level, Northern Taiwan
  • Beginner group (R1–R7): no prior formal interpreting training; L1 Chinese, B-lang English
  • Intermediate group (A1–A7): ≥2 semesters of interpreting training (note-taking, reformulation, delivery)
  • All Chinese native speakers; all sufficient English for academic speech (B1-B2)
  • Voluntary participation; written consent obtained
3.3Experimental Materials Design
  • English speech on AI's influence on education (Singapore professor, ~10 min)
  • Divided into 4 segments (~90–120 sec each) using CapCut
  • Difficulty: B1–B2 (CEFR), verified by Text Inspector
  • ASR subtitles auto-generated by Verbal.ai
  • Fixed subtitle order: (1) No subtitle → (2) EN → (3) EN+ZH bilingual → (4) ZH
  • Rationale for order: baseline → source-lang support → max support → target-lang support
3.4Experimental Procedure Flow
  • Individual sessions, controlled classroom, same equipment for all
  • Step 1: Briefing + informed consent
  • Step 2: Watch speech segment with subtitle condition
  • Step 3: Consecutive interpret into Chinese (iPhone audio recorded)
  • Step 4: Complete Paas Cognitive Load Scale (1–9)
  • Steps 2–4 repeated × 4 (once per subtitle condition)
  • Step 5: Post-task questionnaire + semi-structured interview
3.5Data Collection
3.5.1 Interpreting Performance
  • Analytic rubric adapted from Cheung & Li (2022), expanded to 4 criteria:
  • Accuracy (0–5): meaning fidelity to source
  • Terminology (0–5): appropriate lexical choices
  • Fluency (0–5): smoothness, pauses, hesitations
  • Delivery (0–5): clarity and comprehensibility
  • Max score per segment: 20 points; scored by single rater (acknowledged as limitation)
3.5.2 Cognitive Load
  • Paas Scale (1992): single-item, 9-point (1=very low effort, 9=very high)
  • Supplementary items: subtitle reliance / subtitle interference / segment difficulty
  • Administered immediately after each segment
3.5.3 ASR Transcription
  • Used as ecological validity tool, not a research variable
  • All participants received same ASR conditions
3.5.4 Qualitative Data
  • Post-task questionnaire: perceptions of subtitle conditions
  • Semi-structured interviews: strategies, experiences, attitudes
  • Supplements and contextualises quantitative findings
3.6Data Analysis Stats
  • Quantitative: SPSS — repeated-measures ANOVA (within-subject: subtitle condition; between-subject: training level)
  • Descriptive stats: mean + SD for performance and cognitive load per condition
  • Group comparisons: beginner vs. intermediate across all 4 conditions
  • Qualitative: thematic analysis of interview responses
  • Mixed triangulation: qualitative findings used to explain quantitative patterns
